# Safeguarding in comms

## Scope:

Applies to **communications decisions involving children, young people, and adults at risk** (clients, residents, patients, survivors, people in crisis). Not a replacement for your organisation's full safeguarding policy. Pair with [`data-protection-for-comms.md`](data-protection-for-comms.md), [`image-and-quote-releases.md`](image-and-quote-releases.md), and [`respectful-language.md`](../language-&-communication/respectful-language.md).

## Excerpt

- **Safeguarding comes before the story.** No image, quote, or case study is worth harm to the person in it.
- Children and vulnerable adults need **guardian consent + age-appropriate assent** plus an extra pair of editorial eyes.
- **Identifiability is cumulative**—first name + diagnosis + neighbourhood is identifying even without a face.
- Assume **copies persist**. What you publish today may resurface in a court case, a new relationship, or a future employer's search.
- Full principles, workflow, and red flags below.

## Before involving at-risk people

Before drafting copy or preparing media involving a person at risk, clarify **who they are, what they understand, and what you owe them**.

### Concrete

- **Age, capacity, current situation** (acute crisis, settled, in aftercare)?
- Who gives **consent**, who gives **assent**, who must **not** be informed (e.g. an abuser)?
- **Identifiability**: which combinations of details let someone recognise them?
- Are there **safety risks** (family, partner, state, community) if they are recognised?

### Meta

- Comms is often **one stakeholder among several**—care, legal, safeguarding leads all weigh in. Respect that, even when deadlines hurt.
- A published piece is **irreversible in effect**, even if deleted.

---

## Purpose

Give comms work a clear safeguarding floor so stories about real people **do not cause new harm**, and so the organisation can stand behind every publication.

---

## 1. Core principles

- **Safety first, story second.**
- **Consent is ongoing, not a moment.** Check again before publication.
- **Identifiability is a spectrum.** "We changed the name" may not be enough.
- **Power matters.** Employees, clients, and volunteers are not in equal positions with the org.
- **Transparent with the person**, confidential outside.

## 2. Who counts as "at risk"

- **Children and young people** (under 18, stricter rules below school age).
- **Adults at risk** by circumstance: in care settings, shelters, prisons, hospitals, refugee accommodation, addiction services, end-of-life care.
- **Adults at risk** by context: survivors of abuse, trafficking, or crime; people with capacity limitations; people in acute crisis.
- **Third parties** named by the interviewee (ex-partners, family members, perpetrators)—they did not consent.

## 3. Consent that holds up

- **Plain language** explanation of what will be published, where, and for how long—see [`easy-read-english.md`](../language-&-communication/easy-read-english.md).
- **Time and space** to decline; not in the middle of a crisis moment.
- **Guardian consent + child assent** for minors; never just the guardian.
- **Support person / advocate** present for vulnerable adults where possible.
- Written, dated, and filed—see [`image-and-quote-releases.md`](image-and-quote-releases.md).

## 4. Identifiability check

Before publishing, ask:

- Could **family, employer, school, local community** identify this person from the combination of details?
- Does the piece reveal **location** (city, neighbourhood, specific service)?
- Is there a **photo**, a **voice clip**, a **unique biographical fact** (rare illness, unusual job)?
- Anonymise by **removing enough details**—not by changing one name.

## 5. What usually needs to go

- Full faces of children in sensitive contexts.
- Case studies with three or more unique locators (age + condition + town + service).
- Quotes naming third parties who did not consent.
- Details that could reach an abuser, a trafficker, or immigration enforcement.
- Medical specifics beyond what the person asked for.

## 6. Workflow

1. **Brief** the editorial team on the safeguarding context before the interview.
2. **Interview** with a support person available, and with consent documented.
3. **Draft** internally; share with the person in a readable format.
4. **Revise** on the person's feedback; revisit consent if the piece shifted.
5. **Publish** only after the **safeguarding lead** has signed off for sensitive cases.
6. **Review** periodically—remove pieces when the purpose has ended or the person withdraws.

## 7. Incident response

- If a published piece causes harm or distress, **take it down first, discuss later**.
- Record: what happened, who was affected, what was changed, who made the decision.
- Learn: update the process so the same gap does not reopen.
- Care: offer the person appropriate support channels, not just a PR response.

## 8. Working with partners, donors, and press

- Do not share raw interview material, photos, or names with donors or partners without the person's consent.
- Press outlets may want more identifying detail than you agreed to—hold the line.
- Funders cannot override a safeguarding decision, even if they fund the project.

## 9. AI and safeguarding

- Do not paste real names, photos, or case details into AI tools you have not cleared.
- Do not generate synthetic images that could be mistaken for real people at risk.
- See [`ai-disclosure-and-policy.md`](ai-disclosure-and-policy.md).

## 10. Red flags

- "It's a great story, we just need a face."
- A guardian keen for publicity the child clearly does not want.
- A deadline that won't allow proper consent.
- A donor naming a specific individual they want featured.
- A "before/after" framing of someone's life that the person did not choose.

---

## Core idea

Safeguarding in comms means **the person in the story is better off after publication than before**. If you cannot honestly say that, the story is not ready—or is not yours to tell.
