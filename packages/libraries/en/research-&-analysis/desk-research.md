# Desk research

## Scope:

Applies to **non-empirical research from existing sources** for comms, policy briefs, stories, and campaigns—web, library databases, official statistics, civil-society reports. Not primary data collection and not peer-reviewed study design. Pair with [`source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md`](source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md), [`notes-and-synthesis.md`](notes-and-synthesis.md), [`interviewing-people.md`](interviewing-people.md).

## Excerpt

- **Start with the question**, not the search box. "What are we actually trying to find out?"
- **Plan your search**: terms, synonyms, languages, sources, dates.
- **Track every source**: URL, title, author, date accessed, where used. Future-you will thank present-you.
- **Triangulate**: any non-trivial claim needs at least two independent sources.
- Full workflow, search patterns, and red flags below.

## Before you start searching

Before opening any browser tab, clarify **the question, the deliverable, and the deadline**.

### Concrete

- **Question** in one sentence. What do we need to know by when?
- **Use**: story, press, grant, internal brief, factsheet, blog post?
- **Audience** of the deliverable—lay, specialist, funder, journalist?
- **Sources** likely: official statistics, peer-reviewed, gray literature, news, civil society?
- **Languages** you can search in.

### Meta

- Desk research expands to fill available time. **Time-box** each phase.
- Rabbit holes are fine in the first pass; close them before the second.

---

## Purpose

Turn a fuzzy question into a small, trustworthy pile of evidence—**fast, well-sourced, and reproducible**.

---

## 1. Phase by phase

1. **Frame** — restate the question; define in/out of scope.
2. **Plan** — 5–10 search terms (plus synonyms), 3–5 source types, time and language limits.
3. **Sweep** — fast breadth: headlines, dates, reports. No reading yet.
4. **Read** — depth on the 5–10 most relevant hits.
5. **Synthesise** — see [`notes-and-synthesis.md`](notes-and-synthesis.md).
6. **Verify** — cross-check against independent sources; see [`source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md`](source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md).
7. **Cite** — complete references before writing.

## 2. Planning the search

- **Search terms**: domain terms, plain-language terms, specialist jargon, acronyms expanded and abbreviated.
- **Operators**: `"exact phrase"`, `-exclude`, `site:domain`, `filetype:pdf`, date filters.
- **Languages**: include local terms (e.g. German and English for EU topics); translate search terms carefully.
- **Time window**: recent for news, wider for context; always note **publication date**.

## 3. Source types (rough hierarchy)

- **Official statistics** (Destatis, Eurostat, OECD, UN, national ministries).
- **Peer-reviewed** studies (via Google Scholar, PubMed, OpenAIRE).
- **Government and regulator** documents.
- **Civil society reports** (NGOs, foundations, think tanks) — note who funds them.
- **Journalism**: quality press, investigative outlets.
- **Social media / blogs**: signal, not source. Useful for leads and voices, not for claims.
- **Wikipedia**: start, not destination. Follow the footnotes.

## 4. What goes in the source log

For every source that might make it into the deliverable:

| Field               | Example                     |
| ------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Title               | _Armutsbericht 2024_        |
| Author / publisher  | Caritas Germany             |
| Date of publication | 2024-10-12                  |
| Date accessed       | 2026-04-17                  |
| URL / DOI           | https://…                   |
| Type                | report / news / data / blog |
| Relevance           | high / medium / low         |
| Used in             | section X of draft          |
| Notes               | raw quote, page, context    |

## 5. Gray literature specifics

- Always capture: who wrote it, who paid for it, methodology, sample size, date.
- Note whether the organisation has an **advocacy position** on the topic—not disqualifying, but important context.
- Download a PDF copy; URLs rot.

## 6. Statistics and data

- Prefer the **original** dataset over a news summary. Each layer of reporting introduces noise.
- Note **definitions** (how is "poverty" measured here?), **base year**, **geography**, **sample**.
- See [`reading-statistics-plainly.md`](reading-statistics-plainly.md).

## 7. Sweeps and focused reads

- **Sweep** mode: skim titles, abstracts, summaries; take minimal notes; flag the promising 10–20.
- **Focused read**: only on the flagged set; read methodology and key findings; extract quotes with page numbers.
- Resist reading top-to-bottom before sweeping—you will over-invest in the first thing you find.

## 8. Dead ends and pivots

- If the question does not have a clean answer, **say so in the deliverable**.
- "We could not find X; here is the closest evidence" is honest and often useful.
- A pivot ("real question is actually…") after a sweep is normal—log the pivot with a date.

## 9. Ethics

- **Paywalled** research: respect the paywall; use institutional access or the author's preprint.
- **Copyright**: quote briefly with attribution; never wholesale-copy.
- **Personal data**: no scraping personal details from forums; see [`data-protection-for-comms.md`](../ethics-&-legal/data-protection-for-comms.md).
- **AI search tools**: verify every claim from the tool's output; treat them as draft leads, never as sources.

## 10. Red flags

- A citation chain that loops back to a single blog post.
- Statistics without a year, source, or definition.
- "According to a study" — which study? Authors? Sample?
- Sources that only agree with your prior hypothesis.
- A single source carrying the weight of a major claim.

---

## Core idea

Desk research is **disciplined curiosity**: know the question, search wide then narrow, log every source, and triangulate the important claims. Speed comes from structure, not from skipping it.
