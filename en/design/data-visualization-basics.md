# Data visualization basics

**Scope:** Applies to **charts and data graphics** in dashboards and reports (web or static); not full statistical analysis or BI tool configuration.

## Excerpt (read this first)

- Use for **choosing chart types**, labeling axes, and **color** that does not distort meaning.
- Prefer **plain language** titles; show **uncertainty** when relevant.
- **Accessibility:** do not rely on color alone for categories; provide table alternative or labels when possible.
- Full file below: chart choice, pitfalls, checklist. NGO context: **impact metrics** without sensationalizing.

## Purpose

This skill helps communicators and designers produce **honest, readable** data visuals for **impact dashboards, annual reports, and internal monitoring**.

## AI / prompt: completeness before drafting

### Concrete

- **Data type** (trend, comparison, part-of-whole, distribution)
- **Audience** (public, board, team)
- **Number format** and **units**

### Meta

- **Risk** of misinterpretation (e.g. cumulative vs per period)

---

## Core rules

### 1. Match chart type to the question

| Question | Often useful |
|----------|----------------|
| Change over time | Line or column |
| Compare categories | Bar (horizontal if labels long) |
| Part of whole (sums to 100%) | Stacked bar or pie only if few segments |
| Correlation | Scatter |

### ❌ Bad

```text
Pie chart with 12 similar slices; 3D effects for “impact.”
```

### ✅ Good

```text
Sorted bar chart for “top 5 programmes by participants”; clear title.
```

---

### 2. Axis and units

- Start at **zero** for bar/column **counts** unless a strong reason to truncate.
- State **units** in axis label or subtitle (e.g. “EUR thousands”).

---

### 3. Color for categories, not for “more green = better”

Avoid implying **moral** quality with a single green–red gradient unless the metric is clearly signed good/bad.

---

### 4. Labels and clutter

- Prefer **direct labels** on or near marks when readable.
- Reduce gridlines; **no** decorative chart junk.

---

### 5. Accessibility

- **Legend** or labels that don’t rely on color alone (patterns or text).
- **Alt text** or **summary** for key takeaway for screen reader users.

---

## Checklist

- [ ] Chart type matches the **question** and data shape
- [ ] Axes, units, and time range are **explicit**
- [ ] No misleading truncation or dual axes without clear explanation
- [ ] Color is **legible** in grayscale and for common color-vision deficiencies
- [ ] Source and date of data **cited** when publishing externally

---

## Final thought

👉 A chart is an **argument**. Make the claim **proportionate** to the data.
