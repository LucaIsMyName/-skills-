# Crisis legal checklist

**Scope:** Applies to **legal and rights-aware steps during a communications crisis** (accusation, incident, bad press, viral post, safeguarding event, data breach). Not a replacement for a lawyer on an active case. Pair with [`crisis-and-sensitive-topics.md`](../language-and-communication/crisis-and-sensitive-topics.md), [`data-protection-for-comms.md`](data-protection-for-comms.md), and [`safeguarding-in-comms.md`](safeguarding-in-comms.md).

## Excerpt (read this first)

- **Silence is a decision.** So is a statement. Both have legal weight—make each one deliberately.
- **Facts first, tone second.** Do not say "we are sorry for X" before you know X happened.
- **Loop in legal early**, not after the first statement is out. A holding line is cheap; a retraction is expensive.
- **Preserve evidence**: logs, emails, versions, analytics. Do not delete in panic.
- **People before press**: affected persons, staff, partners are contacted **before** public statements where possible.
- Full checklist and anti-patterns below.

## AI / prompt: before communicating in a crisis

Before drafting a statement, clarify **what is known, what is alleged, what is confidential, and who must sign off**.

### Concrete

- **Known facts** vs. **allegations** vs. **speculation**—mark each.
- Are there **legal constraints** (ongoing investigation, court order, employment law, GDPR)?
- Who is **affected** and **how will they hear it** (direct, press, social)?
- Which **channels** do we control, which do we not?

### Meta

- "Getting ahead of the story" rarely survives contact with facts. Correctness beats speed.
- Keep an audit trail: who approved which version, and when.

## Purpose

Add a **legal and rights layer** to crisis comms so statements are honest, compliant, and do not make the incident worse.

---

## 1. Core principles

- **Do not concede liability** in public statements—express care without accepting facts you cannot verify.
- **Do not identify** individuals (survivors, suspects, staff, minors) without legal and safeguarding clearance.
- **Separate** what you know, what you are investigating, and what is speculation.
- **Coordinate** legal, comms, safeguarding, HR, and leadership in one decision line.
- **Document** every decision and every draft.

## 2. First hour

- [ ] Convene a **small incident group** (leadership, legal, comms, safeguarding, HR as relevant).
- [ ] Agree **a single spokesperson**; everyone else declines and forwards.
- [ ] Write a **holding statement** (facts, care, next update time)—see [`crisis-and-sensitive-topics.md`](../language-and-communication/crisis-and-sensitive-topics.md).
- [ ] **Preserve** relevant records (emails, logs, analytics, social posts).
- [ ] Notify **affected people** directly where feasible before public channels.

## 3. Information triage

For every fact under consideration:

| Label | Use publicly? |
|---|---|
| Verified | Yes, carefully |
| Alleged | Attribute the source; do not repeat as fact |
| Under investigation | Mention the process, not a conclusion |
| Speculation | Do not publish |

Keep this table **visibly** in the incident doc.

## 4. Legal red lines

- **Defamation**: avoid stating contested facts about named third parties.
- **Data protection**: no personal data in statements without a lawful basis (and usually not even then—see [`data-protection-for-comms.md`](data-protection-for-comms.md)).
- **Employment**: comments about staff under investigation can breach contract and employment law.
- **Ongoing investigations**: respect sub judice / court restrictions.
- **Safeguarding**: children and vulnerable adults are not identifiable, ever, without explicit clearance.

## 5. Apologies and acknowledgements

- "We are sorry for what X is going through" ≠ "We accept that Y caused it."
- Care language is appropriate early: "we take this seriously", "we will investigate", "our priority is the affected person".
- **Specific admissions** wait for evidence and legal review.

## 6. Social channels

- **Pause** scheduled marketing on the crisis topic and adjacent areas.
- **Monitor** but do not argue with individual posts.
- **Correct factual errors** once, with a link to the statement.
- **Screenshot** your own statements and third-party posts that may disappear.

## 7. Staff and volunteers

- Issue **internal guidance** before external statements: what staff may say, what they must refer on.
- Remind staff of **confidentiality** and social media policy.
- Provide **support** (EAP, pastoral care, line manager check-in) for those closest to the incident.

## 8. Data breach specifics

- Data protection authority notice: usually within **72 hours** in the EU.
- Affected individuals: notify **without undue delay** if there is a high risk to them.
- Log: what happened, who was affected, what data, what mitigation—see [`data-protection-for-comms.md`](data-protection-for-comms.md).

## 9. When the story is over

- Post-incident review: what happened, what we did, what we change.
- Update **policies** (comms, AI, safeguarding, data protection) based on findings.
- **Close the loop** with affected people and staff.
- Retire the holding materials; archive the incident folder with restricted access.

## 10. Red flags

- A single person drafting, approving, and publishing under time pressure.
- "Just get it out on social now, we'll correct later."
- Blaming a named individual in a public statement.
- Deleting social posts or records to tidy the story.
- Promising outcomes of an investigation before it has begun.

---

## Core idea

In a crisis, comms is **one of several disciplines** sharing the pen. Slow, accurate, coordinated statements beat fast, quotable, contested ones—especially months later in a court, a review, or a funder meeting.
