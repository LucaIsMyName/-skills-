# Easy Read English (plain, accessible English)

**Scope:** Applies to rewriting text into **plain, accessible English**; not certified Easy Read formats with symbols, layout, and user testing unless the user explicitly asks.

## Excerpt 

- Use to **simplify** vocabulary and sentences for literacy, EAL readers, or general clarity.
- **Do not** change legal meaning, numbers, or required terms without explicit instruction—**ask** if unsure.
- Prefer short sentences, one idea per sentence, consistent terms, active voice where clear.
- Full file below: rules, workflow, mistakes to avoid, and delivery checklist.

## Purpose

This document explains how to **rewrite or translate any source text** into **clear, accessible English**—structured, rule-based, and appropriate for readers with **different literacy levels**, **English as an additional language**, and **general audiences** who need plain wording.

The label **“Easy Read English”** is used here for **text-level simplification** (vocabulary, sentences, structure). It aligns with **international plain-language principles** (e.g. **ISO 24495-1**, which applies across languages) and literacy-friendly English. The site [Easy Read English](https://www.easyreadenglish.com/) focuses on **reading instruction** (e.g. syllables, stories); This document is about **producing easy-to-read text**, not replacing a full reading curriculum.

👉 If the user wants a **specific “Easy Read” accessibility format** (very short lines, images, testing with readers), treat that as an **extra layout and process requirement**—ask before assuming.

## completeness before drafting

Before rewriting, gather **concrete and meta information**. If something material is missing, **ask**—do not change legal meaning, numbers, or quotes without explicit instruction.

### Concrete

- **Source text** or topic (full text, excerpt, bullet points)?
- **Task:** simplify English only, or **translate into English** and simplify?
- **Locale:** e.g. en-GB, en-US; fixed spellings for names and terms
- **Must-use or must-not-change** terms (legal, medical, policy)
- **Length:** summary, same scope, or maximum word count?

### Meta

- **Audience** (general public, children’s parents, older adults, EAL readers, etc.)
- **Context:** urgent vs. calm reading (affects sentence length and signposting)
- Whether the user wants **plain language** only or also an **“Easy Read” document format** (short lines, images) — default here is **plain accessible English text**
- **Respect:** do not omit information that is needed for decisions without agreement

## Core rules

### Sentences

- Prefer **one main idea per sentence**.
- **Avoid** long subordination chains; split into two sentences instead.
- Aim for an **average** sentence length typical of plain-language guidance (often **~15–20 words** as a practical target; adjust for tone and genre—legal text may need slightly longer sentences but still **one idea** each).
- Use **lists** when steps or criteria are easier to scan than a paragraph.

### Words

- Prefer **frequent, concrete words**; replace jargon when possible.
- If a **technical term** is required: define it once in simple words, or give a short gloss at first use.
- **Be consistent:** use one term for one concept (avoid elegant variation that confuses readers).
- Prefer **active voice** when it makes the actor clear (“We will send the letter” vs. “The letter will be sent”).

### Structure

- **Lead with the main message**; then supporting detail.
- Use **informative headings** and **short paragraphs**.
- **Headline and body copy must match**—each heading reflects what is in the section below.
- **Signpost** logic (“First…”, “Next…”, “If you need help…”).

### Tone and ethics

- **Respectful and direct**—plain language is not “talking down.”
- Do **not** soften or remove **legally required** or **safety-critical** information to make the text “simpler.” Simplify **and** keep substance; flag trade-offs to the user if needed.

---

## Rewriting workflow

1. State the **core message** of the source in one plain sentence (internally).
2. **Outline** sections; move **most important information first**.
3. Rewrite **sentence by sentence**: shorten, de-clutter, swap difficult words.
4. Add **brief definitions** for unavoidable jargon.
5. **Second pass:** check sentence length, **term consistency**, and whether a reader could act on the text.
6. Run the **checklist** below.

---

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Swapping terms for “variety” and breaking **consistency**.
- Turning everything into **telegraphic bullets** when a short paragraph carries the logic better.
- Confusing This document with **simplified readers for language teaching** (e.g. graded readers)—those are **different products** with level labels.
- **Inventing facts**, **distorting numbers**, or **fabricating quotes**.

---

## Checklist before delivery

- Is the **main point** obvious in the first lines?
- Are sentences mostly **short** and **one idea** each?
- Are **key terms** consistent and explained where needed?
- Is the tone **respectful** and **clear**?
- Are **numbers, dates, legal requirements**, and **names** accurate?
- Has the user confirmed if they need **plain text only** vs. a full **Easy Read** layout process?

---

## Short example (illustrative)

**Before (heavy):**

> Eligibility for the hardship fund shall be assessed in accordance with the criteria set out in Schedule 2, having regard to the applicant’s circumstances and any supporting documentation submitted within the prescribed period.

**After (plain):**

> We check whether you can get money from the hardship fund. We use the rules in Schedule 2. We look at your situation and the papers you send us on time.
