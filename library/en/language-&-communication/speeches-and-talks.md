# Speeches and talks

**Scope:** Applies to **scripting and delivering spoken content**—keynotes, opening remarks, panel statements, ceremonies, short video addresses. Not interviewing (see [`interview-preparation.md`](interview-preparation.md)) and not presentation slides (see [`slides-and-presentations.md`](../design/slides-and-presentations.md)). Pair with [`storytelling.md`](storytelling.md).

## Excerpt
- **One audience, one goal, one takeaway.** Everything else is support.
- **Spoken language is short.** Sentences fit on one breath; lists fit on one hand.
- **Stories beat slides.** Two concrete stories outperform twelve abstract points.
- **Time is a hard limit.** Cut to fit—never run over on someone else's stage.
- Full workflow, script patterns, and delivery rules below.

## Before drafting a talk

Before writing, clarify **who is in the room**, **what changes afterwards**, and **how long you have**.

### Concrete

- **Audience**: size, background, why they came, language level.
- **Occasion**: keynote, closing, ceremonial, commemorative, product launch, fundraiser, council session.
- **Duration** and where in the programme (after dinner, before coffee, right after a serious segment).
- **Setting**: lectern vs. stage walk, teleprompter vs. cards, live vs. recorded, translated or not.
- **Goal** in one sentence: inform, move, invite, honour, mourn, celebrate.

### Meta

- "Interesting for me" is not a goal. **What do they do next?** is a goal.
- Short beats clever. "Memorable" starts with "understood".

## Purpose

Draft and deliver a talk that is **clear, humane, and on time**—one where the audience remembers the core point and the speaker stays in control of the room.

---

## 1. Structure that works

- **Open** (≈10%): hook—story, image, question, single fact—and signal the **one takeaway**.
- **Body** (≈75%): three beats (rarely more). Each beat: short claim → concrete example → implication.
- **Close** (≈15%): callback to the opening, restate the takeaway, a **call to feel, think, or do** one thing.

## 2. Write for the ear

- **Contractions**: "we're", "don't", "it's". Reading aloud should not sound robotic.
- **Short sentences.** If you lose breath, the audience loses attention.
- **Concrete words**: a person, a place, a date—over "stakeholders", "ecosystem", "impact".
- **Read aloud while editing.** Anything you cannot say without stumbling, rewrite.

### Bad: write for the ear

```text
Our multifaceted initiative leverages intersectoral partnerships in order to holistically address the structural determinants…
```

### Good: write for the ear

```text
We work with three hospitals and six schools in one district. Together we fix what one of us cannot fix alone.
```

## 3. Signposting

- "There are three things I want to leave you with."
- "First… Second… Finally…"
- "Let me show you what that looks like on a Monday morning."
- "Before I sit down, one last thought."

Spoken audiences can't scroll back—tell them where they are.

## 4. Stories in spoken form

- **One protagonist, one moment, one change.** See [`storytelling.md`](storytelling.md).
- Enter **late**, leave **early**: no setup paragraph, no moral speech at the end.
- Use present tense for scenes ("She walks in, looks at the form, and asks—"); past tense for analysis.
- Attribute carefully—get consent and accuracy; see [`writing-a-story.md`](writing-a-story.md).

## 5. Numbers sparingly

- Pick **one** number, name it, contextualise it.
- Pair with a human image ("One in four—that is six people in this row").
- Never stack three statistics in a row; the audience forgets the first before you finish the third.

## 6. Transitions and pacing

- **Pause** at the end of a big sentence for two beats.
- **Speed up** on narrative, **slow down** on the key line.
- **Repeat** the takeaway in different words three times across the talk—opening, middle, close.

## 7. Delivery

- **Stand still on the key line**; move on transitions.
- **Make eye contact** with three zones of the room, not one.
- **Hands** open and visible; the lectern is not a shield.
- **Breathe between sentences**; cardiac rhythm beats verbal waterfall.
- **Smile once** early—permission for the audience to relax.

## 8. Stage, mic, light

- **Arrive early**, walk the stage, test the mic.
- **Know the lights**: you may not see the audience; talk to the lights.
- **Feet grounded**; weight even. No rocking.
- **Clip mic** placement: below the collarbone, away from fabric noise.

## 9. Translation and accessibility

- If your talk is translated live, **pause between sentences**; no nested clauses.
- Provide the script to interpreters **in advance** if at all possible.
- Offer captions/transcript for hybrid or recorded talks—see [`accessibility-for-comms.md`](accessibility-for-comms.md).
- Describe visuals you refer to ("Behind me is a photo of…") for blind audience members.

## 10. Ceremonial and sensitive occasions

- **Mood first.** Do not start with "delighted to be here" at a memorial.
- **Name the occasion** clearly and kindly.
- **No jokes** unless agreed with the organisers for that audience.
- **Short**. Five honest minutes beat fifteen rhetorical ones.
- Coordinate with the order of proceedings; do not surprise the hosts.

## 11. Cutting to fit

- **140 words per minute** is a comfortable baseline; read your draft aloud with a stopwatch.
- Cut adjectives, throat-clearing openings ("It's a real pleasure…"), redundant examples.
- If you must cut late, cut **examples** before **beats**; keep the structure.

## 12. What not to do

- **Don't read your slides aloud.** If it is on the slide, say something else.
- **Don't apologise** for nerves, the topic, or the time. Just start.
- **Don't end on a thank-you.** End on your takeaway, _then_ thank.
- **Don't ad-lib** high-stakes lines. Write them.
- **Don't go long.** The most generous thing a speaker can do is finish early.

---

## Core idea

A good talk is **a gift of attention given back to the audience as clarity**. Know your one takeaway, say it three times, and leave on time.
