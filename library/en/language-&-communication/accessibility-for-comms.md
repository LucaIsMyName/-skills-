# Accessibility for communications

**Scope:** Applies to **communications teams only** (PR, marketing, social media, internal comms, campaigns): **how you write, brief, speak, and plan messages** so more people can understand and use them. **Not** for developers, CMS markup, or product UI code—use design skills (e.g. [`design-basics.md`](../design/design-basics.md)) for implementation details.

## Excerpt
- Use when **wording content** for newsletters, press, social, events, podcasts, or **briefing** designers/web colleagues.
- Focus: **plain language**, **respectful wording**, **image descriptions** (what to say in words), **link phrasing**, **video captions** as a comms deliverable (script, briefing), **accessible speaking** (clear pace, describing visuals aloud when presenting).
- **Do not** invent legal accessibility compliance; flag when **legal or web team** sign-off is needed.
- Full file below: rules and checklist. Pair with [`respectful-language.md`](respectful-language.md) and [`easy-read-english.md`](easy-read-english.md).

## Purpose

This document helps **nonprofit communicators** make content **more inclusive** through **language and process**—not by replacing accessibility engineering or WCAG audits.

## completeness before drafting

### Concrete

- **Channel** (email, social post, speech notes, press release, event)
- **Images:** which need a **written description** for audiences who cannot see them?
- **Video/audio:** will there be **captions or a transcript**? Who provides them?
- **Links:** destination and how you phrase the link in copy

### Meta

- **Audience** (general public, stressed readers, mixed languages)
- **What comms owns vs web/IT** in your organization—**ask** if unclear

## Core rules

### 1. Image descriptions (for editors and briefs)

Communicators should supply **short, useful descriptions** of meaningful images so they can be published accessibly. Write what someone needs to know **if they cannot see the image**—not the file name.

### Bad: core rules

```text
Photo: IMG_4492.jpg
```

### Good: core rules

```text
Description for editors: A volunteer hands a food parcel to a visitor outside the community centre entrance.
```

---

### 2. Decorative or stock images

If an image is **only decorative**, say so in your brief (“no informational content—decorative only”) so web colleagues can handle display options. You do not need to write HTML.

---

### 3. Clear structure in copy (titles and subheadings)

In drafts and newsletters, use **one clear main title** and **subheadings in logical order**—like an outline—so readers and editors can follow. Pass the same structure to web teams; **markup and coding are their job**.

**Headline and body copy must match:** each heading should describe the section below it (no empty promise).

### Bad: core rules

```text
Tiny sub-label “Programmes” buried under a huge unrelated banner title; three different topics in one wall of text.
```

### Good: core rules

```text
Main title: Our work in 2025
Subheading: Education and mentoring
Short paragraphs under each subheading.
```

---

### 4. Link text: meaningful in the sentence

Readers should understand **where the link goes** without relying on “click here.”

### Bad: core rules

```text
Click here for more information.
```

### Good: core rules

```text
Read our 2025 impact report (PDF).
```

---

### 5. Video and audio: plan captions and transcripts

- **Captions/subtitles** for spoken content: assign **who writes or orders** them (comms script, agency, platform tool).
- **Podcasts/events:** offer **transcripts or summaries** when your org can sustain them.
- **Speaking:** if you show a chart on stage, **say the main takeaway out loud** for people who cannot see the slide.

---

### 6. Speaking and events

- Use **plain language**; explain acronyms on first use.
- **Moderators:** repeat audience questions into the microphone when needed.
- **Slides:** large type and high contrast are comms/design choices—brief your slide designer (see [`slides-and-presentations.md`](../design/slides-and-presentations.md)).

---

## Checklist before publish or event

- [ ] **Image descriptions** drafted for meaningful photos (or marked decorative in the brief)
- [ ] **Titles and subheadings** form a clear outline in the copy
- [ ] **Links** say where they go; no vague “click here”
- [ ] **Video/audio:** captioning or transcript **planned** (owner named) or explicitly out of scope
- [ ] **Live content:** speaker briefed to **verbalize** key visuals where relevant
- [ ] **Escalation:** legal/web accessibility standards flagged for the right team, not guessed in copy

---

## Core idea
 In comms, accessibility starts with **respectful, clear language** and **good briefs**. Technical implementation belongs with **web and design colleagues**—This document keeps your lane **writing and speaking**.
