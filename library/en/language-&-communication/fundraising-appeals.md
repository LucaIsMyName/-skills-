# Fundraising appeals

**Scope:** Applies to **donor-facing appeals** in email, letter, landing page, and campaign copy for nonprofit and social-sector work. Not grant applications (see [`grant-writing.md`](grant-writing.md)) and not major-gift negotiations. Pair with [`writing-a-story.md`](writing-a-story.md), [`respectful-language.md`](respectful-language.md), and [`newsletter-and-email.md`](newsletter-and-email.md).

## Excerpt 

- **One person, one need, one ask.** Appeals are not annual reports—trim everything else.
- **Dignity over dramatics.** People in the appeal are portrayed as **agents**, not "cases"; your org is a partner, not a saviour.
- **Concrete impact beats vague emotion.** "€20 pays for a week of after-school meals for Leila's class" outperforms "your gift changes lives".
- **Ask plainly** and **early**. The donor should never have to search for what you want them to do.
- No guilt bait, no poverty porn, no implied coercion. Full rules, structure, and anti-patterns below.

## Before drafting an appeal

Before writing, clarify **who is being asked, for what, on behalf of whom, and with what consent**.

### Concrete

- **Cause and campaign**: what specifically are we raising for?
- **Beneficiaries**: named, anonymous, composite—what is their consent status?
- **Concrete ask**: amount(s), unit of impact, channel (one-off, monthly, legacy)?
- **Audience segment**: first-time, lapsed, monthly, major donor?
- **Deadline / match / urgency**: real or manufactured? If manufactured, rewrite.

### Meta

- The people in the appeal come first, not the copy. If you would not want to be portrayed this way, neither do they.
- Donors are adults. Honesty raises more, and keeps, more than emotional manipulation.

---

## Purpose

Write appeals that **move people to give**, respect everyone portrayed, and tell the truth—without trading dignity for response rates.

---

## 1. Structure of a strong appeal

1. **Hook** — one concrete person, scene, or moment (3–4 sentences).
2. **The need** — why it matters now, briefly.
3. **The work** — what your org does, in one paragraph, with one example of impact.
4. **The ask** — a clear amount with a unit of impact, twice if needed.
5. **The close** — reassurance (privacy, frequency, stewardship), a thank-you in advance.
6. **P.S.** — repeat the ask in one line; people read the P.S.

## 2. The hook: a real moment

- One person (with consent) or one scene. **Not** a stack of statistics.
- Present tense for vividness; past tense for reflection.
- Small detail: the colour of the jacket, the name of the afternoon class, the weight of the bag.

### Bad

```text
Across the country, thousands of children lack access to nutritious meals, posing significant challenges to their long-term development outcomes.
```

### Good

```text
It's 3:40pm on a Tuesday. Leila finishes school, walks to our community kitchen, and eats the first hot meal of her day. Her mother works two shifts and can't be home until 9.
```

## 3. Dignity rules

- People in appeals are **agents** with choices, not "the poor", "the needy", "the voiceless".
- **No before/after** framing that strips someone's history. Their life did not start with your programme.
- **No pity close-ups**: tears, wounds, filth. Invite the photographer to find strength, not suffering.
- **Consent documented** for every name and face; see [`image-and-quote-releases.md`](../ethics-safeguarding-legal/image-and-quote-releases.md).
- **Anonymise** (composite, pseudonym, role) when safety requires—and **say so**.

## 4. The ask: concrete and plain

- Amounts **with a unit of impact**: "€20 pays for one week of after-school meals" (honest average, not cherry-picked).
- Offer **two or three amounts**, with the middle one as the suggested default.
- **Monthly option** labelled clearly ("Give €10 a month" not "Become a Guardian Circle Member").
- **One primary CTA** per page or email. Secondary links to FAQ, privacy, unsubscribe.

### Bad

```text
Please consider supporting our mission in whatever way you can.
```

### Good

```text
Give €20 today—that's a week of after-school meals for one child like Leila.
Or set up €10 a month so the kitchen is open all year.
```

## 5. Numbers with honesty

- **Round** where it helps readability, but never round up to mislead.
- **Name the unit** ("per child per week", not just "per unit").
- Pair with a human image or one story—never a block of statistics.
- If the €20 figure is an average, say "on average"—donors forgive honesty, not spin.

## 6. Urgency without fake deadlines

- Real urgency: winter is coming, the lease ends March, the match runs out Friday at 18:00.
- Fake urgency ("Last chance!") once or twice teaches donors to ignore you.
- Matched gifts: only claim a match you can document.

## 7. Stewardship and reassurance

- Short line on **privacy**: "We will not share your details; you can opt out any time."
- **Frequency promise**: how often you'll ask again, and how often you'll report back.
- **Thank** in advance in a specific way; a real thank-you follow-up is the next letter.

## 8. Channel-specific notes

### Email

- Subject line names the person or the ask, not clickbait; see [`newsletter-and-email.md`](newsletter-and-email.md).
- Preheader extends the promise.
- One CTA above the fold, one at the bottom, one in the P.S.

### Direct mail letter

- Handwritten-style signature block with a real name and role.
- P.S. is the most-read line after the opening—repeat the ask.
- Include the reply form with the amounts pre-printed.

### Landing page

- Headline + subheadline + hero image in one glance; ask above the fold.
- FAQ below the fold: "Is my gift secure? How is it spent? Can I cancel a monthly gift?"
- Social proof if honest (numbers served, testimonials with consent), never fabricated.

### Major donor proposal

- Longer, more detail, personalised to the donor's known interests.
- Budget breakdown with ranges; named contact for a follow-up call.

## 9. Legal and ethical floor

- **No personal data** in appeals without clear consent—see [`data-protection-for-comms.md`](../ethics-safeguarding-legal/data-protection-for-comms.md).
- **Minors and vulnerable adults**: see [`safeguarding-in-comms.md`](../ethics-safeguarding-legal/safeguarding-in-comms.md).
- **Donor data** is sacred: opt-in, clear unsubscribe, honest about sharing (usually: do not share).
- **Tax receipts / regulations**: follow the charity law in your jurisdiction.
- **AI use**: disclose synthetic imagery or substantially AI-written text—see [`ai-disclosure-and-policy.md`](../ethics-safeguarding-legal/ai-disclosure-and-policy.md).

## 10. What not to do

- "Without your gift, this child will…" (coercive, often untrue).
- Stock photos of anonymous crying children.
- Implied exclusivity ("Only 50 spots left to save a life").
- Amounts with no unit of impact.
- An appeal that never reports back. Tell donors what happened next.

---

## Core idea

A good appeal says honestly: **here is one person, here is what changes with your help, here is what we do not do without you**. Dignity is not a tax on response rates; over time, it is why donors stay.
