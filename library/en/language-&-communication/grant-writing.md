# Grant writing

**Scope:** Applies to **formal grant applications, project outlines, and funding proposals** for nonprofits and social-sector organisations—EU, national, foundation, or institutional calls. Not legal review of grant contracts, not major-donor cultivation (see [`fundraising-appeals.md`](fundraising-appeals.md)), not full financial modelling by an accountant. Pair with [`literature-summarising.md`](../research-&-analysis/literature-summarising.md), [`desk-research.md`](../research-&-analysis/desk-research.md), and [`writing-a-story.md`](writing-a-story.md) for narrative sections.

## Excerpt
- **Match the call.** Answer the funder’s questions in their order; do not paste a generic project description into every form.
- **Outcomes, not vibes.** What will change, for whom, by when, and how you will know—aligned to the call’s indicators.
- **Budget = story.** Line items should map to activities and staff time; unexplained round numbers erode trust.
- **Evidence, not hype.** Cite sources for statistics; flag uncertainties; never invent partners, approvals, or past results.
- Full structure, red flags, and checklist below.

## Before drafting a proposal

Before generating text, gather **call text, eligibility, deadlines, and internal facts**. Do not fabricate co-funding, letters of support, or registration numbers.

### Concrete

- **Call ID / programme name** and link to official guidelines.
- **Eligibility**: legal form, geography, thematic fit, co-funding rules.
- **Requested format**: page limits, font, annexes, CV rules, budget template.
- **Project**: aim, target group, activities, timeline, team, partners (real names and roles).
- **Numbers**: budget draft, match funding, FTE—only as provided or clearly marked as placeholders.

### Meta

- Funders read dozens of applications. **Clarity and fit** beat rhetorical flourish.
- If a required field is missing from your inputs, **ask**—do not fill with generic NGO boilerplate.

---

## Purpose

Produce proposals that are **eligible, complete, and honest**—clear about what you will do, what it costs, and how success will be measured.

---

## 1. Read the call before you write

- Highlight **mandatory criteria**, **scoring criteria**, and **ineligibility** clauses.
- Note **definitions** (e.g. “disadvantaged youth”, “small organisation”)—use the funder’s terms.
- Extract **required annexes** early; lead times for signatures and audits kill deadlines.

---

## 2. Typical proposal skeleton

Adapt to the template, but most proposals include:

1. **Summary** — problem, objective, approach, expected change (often character-limited).
2. **Context / need** — evidence-based; cite sources; see [`source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md`](../research-&-analysis/source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md).
3. **Objectives and outcomes** — SMART where the call expects it; distinguish outputs from outcomes.
4. **Methodology / activities** — what you will do, when, with whom; realistic workload.
5. **Partnership** — roles, contributions, letters of support only if real and requested.
6. **Equality, safeguarding, environment** — only if asked; answer specifically, not with generic statements.
7. **Monitoring and evaluation** — indicators, data sources, reporting rhythm.
8. **Budget and justification** — line items tied to activities; explain co-funding.
9. **Organisational capacity** — track record, governance, financial stability—truthfully.

---

## 3. Budget discipline

- Every major cost line should **trace to an activity** or role.
- Separate **staff**, **services**, **equipment**, **overheads** as the template requires.
- **Co-funding** must be real (cash or in-kind) and documented—do not treat “hope” as match.

---

## 4. Tone and ethics

- **Professional, not promotional.** Avoid superlatives unless you can substantiate them.
- **People-first language** for beneficiaries; align with [`respectful-language.md`](respectful-language.md).
- **Data protection**: personal data in proposals only when necessary; see [`data-protection-for-comms.md`](../ethics-&-legal/data-protection-for-comms.md).

---

## 5. Red flags

- Copy-paste from old proposals with outdated stats or partners.
- “We will empower communities” without concrete activities or indicators.
- Budget that does not add up or ignores co-funding rules.
- Missing signatures or annexes at submission time.

---

## Checklist (before submit)

- [ ] Every mandatory field completed; annex list complete.
- [ ] Page and format limits respected.
- [ ] Numbers and years checked against sources.
- [ ] Budget internally consistent with narrative.
- [ ] Partners and quotes confirmed in writing where required.
- [ ] Proofread; one reader who did not write it.

---

## Core idea

A strong grant application is **a precise answer to a precise question**—not a brochure about your organisation.
