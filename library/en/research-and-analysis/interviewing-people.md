# Interviewing people (research)

**Scope:** Applies to **research and reporting interviews**—subject-matter experts, practitioners, affected people, officials—to gather information for stories, briefs, and reports. Not media training for spokespeople (see [`interview-preparation.md`](../language-and-communication/interview-preparation.md)). Pair with [`writing-a-story.md`](../language-and-communication/writing-a-story.md), [`respectful-language.md`](../language-and-communication/respectful-language.md), [`safeguarding-in-comms.md`](../ethics-safeguarding-legal/safeguarding-in-comms.md), [`data-protection-for-comms.md`](../ethics-safeguarding-legal/data-protection-for-comms.md).

## Excerpt (read this first)

- **The interview is theirs, not yours.** You lead the questions; they own the story.
- **Consent before content.** Explain purpose, use, and the person's rights *before* questions, and confirm again at the end.
- **Listen more than you talk.** Aim for a 20/80 speaking ratio—with pauses as a feature.
- **Short, open questions.** "What happened next?" beats "So when you felt frustrated, what did you then decide to do about the funding?"
- Full workflow, question types, recording rules, and ethics below.

## AI / prompt: before scheduling

Before an interview, clarify **who, why, how long, and under what terms**.

### Concrete

- **Purpose** and final use (publication, internal, quoted, background).
- **Topic** and rough territory; is anything off-limits?
- **Language** of the interview; interpreter needed?
- **Format** (in person, phone, video), duration, location.
- **Consent** for recording, for quoting by name, for photos.
- **Risks** for the person (safety, job, legal, stigma)?

### Meta

- If you would not want to be asked these questions in this way, rewrite them.
- Prepare thoroughly so you can **improvise kindly** in the room.

## Purpose

Conduct interviews that collect **accurate, textured, usable** material while treating the person with respect and protecting them from harm.

---

## 1. Before

- **Research** the person and topic; come informed, not dependent on them for basics.
- **Draft** a topic map with 8–12 potential questions; group by theme.
- **Define** non-negotiable facts you need, and "nice to have" texture.
- **Send in advance**: purpose, your org, likely topics, logistics, consent form.
- **Consent**: plain language, specific to use; see [`image-and-quote-releases.md`](../ethics-safeguarding-legal/image-and-quote-releases.md).
- **Safety check** for sensitive topics (support person, quiet space, time of day).

## 2. Opening the room

- **Introduce** yourself, the piece, how long you will take.
- **Reaffirm consent**: recording, quoting, photos; what happens if they change their mind mid-interview.
- **Start low-stakes**: a warm-up question to set tone ("Thanks for coming—how was your morning?").
- **Signal**: they can skip any question; they can go off-record with prior agreement.

## 3. Question types

- **Open**: "What happened next?", "What did that feel like?"—elicit stories.
- **Closed**: "Which year was that?"—confirm facts.
- **Probing**: "You said X. Can you tell me more?"—deepen.
- **Concrete**: "Describe the room", "Walk me through your day"—grounding.
- **Recap**: "So, to make sure I've got it, you…"—verify.
- **Silence**: after a big answer, wait; the next sentence is often the real one.

Avoid **double-barrelled** ("What did you feel and what did you do?") and **leading** ("That must have been awful, right?") questions.

## 4. Listening

- **Eye contact, open body**; notes visible but not buried in the notebook.
- Let **pauses** breathe—5 seconds feels long to you, not to the person.
- **Paraphrase back**: "So it sounds like…" shows you followed and catches misunderstandings.
- **Follow the energy**—if a topic charges up, stay with it; do not hop to the next script item.

## 5. Sensitive topics

- Ask permission to ask: "Would it be ok if I ask about X?"
- Offer **outs**: "You don't have to answer."
- **Do not dig** into trauma without an agreed purpose and the person's clear choice.
- Notice **signs** of distress; pause, offer water, offer to stop.
- **Never promise** outcomes (legal, financial, policy) in exchange for participation.
- Pair with [`safeguarding-in-comms.md`](../ethics-safeguarding-legal/safeguarding-in-comms.md).

## 6. Recording and notes

- **Always record** for accuracy, with consent. Backup recorder recommended.
- Take **sparse notes**: names, numbers, timestamps, "must verify"—not full dictation.
- Note **non-verbal**: silence, laughter, posture changes.
- Timestamp especially good quotes for quick retrieval.

## 7. Accuracy safeguards

- **Spell names**, confirm titles and roles during the interview.
- **Repeat numbers** back.
- **Ask for sources** on contested facts ("How do you know that?").
- **Offer fact-check**: "I'll check quotes with you before publishing" when appropriate.

## 8. Power, status, and language

- Notice imbalances: journalist/researcher vs. client, funded org vs. grassroots, authority vs. person affected.
- **Meet the person in their language** where possible; use an interpreter otherwise.
- Avoid jargon; use the words the person uses.
- Do not interrupt to "correct" lived experience with a statistic.

## 9. Closing

- "Is there anything I should have asked?"—often yields the best quote.
- **Recap** the use and timeline.
- **Re-confirm consent** explicitly ("Is it still okay to quote this and share the photo?").
- **Thank**—and follow up with a note or link when the piece is out.

## 10. After

- **Transcribe** or at least index the recording while still fresh.
- **Log** the interview: date, participant, consent scope, key quotes, follow-ups.
- **Send** quotes for check if agreed; do not let checks become rewrites of the piece.
- **Take care of yourself** after hard interviews; debrief with a colleague.

## 11. Red flags

- Interview in a place the person cannot leave freely.
- Guardians signing for an adult who is capable of consenting.
- Interviewee in acute crisis; push to "get the material" anyway.
- A handler who coaches the answers.
- Promises of anonymity you cannot keep (small orgs, identifying details).

---

## Core idea

A good interview is an exchange of **attention for truth**. You prepare, you listen, you protect the person—and the material that comes back carries life that no desk source can match.
