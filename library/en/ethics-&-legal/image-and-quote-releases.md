# Image and quote releases

**Scope:** Applies to **getting, recording, and respecting permission** to use photos, video, audio, and quotes of identifiable people in communications. Pair with [`photography-for-ngos.md`](../media-&-production/photography-for-ngos.md), [`data-protection-for-comms.md`](data-protection-for-comms.md) and [`safeguarding-in-comms.md`](safeguarding-in-comms.md); for editorial craft see [`writing-a-story.md`](../language-&-communication/writing-a-story.md).

## Excerpt 

- **No recognisable person, no quote, no publication without documented consent.** Verbal "ok" on set is not enough.
- A release must be **specific** (what), **scoped** (where, how long, which channels), **informed** (in the person's language and reading level), and **revocable**.
- **Minors and vulnerable adults** need a guardian's consent **and** the person's assent; treat any uncertainty as a no.
- The release is a **snapshot in time**—later changes in context (political shift, legal case, personal situation) can require re-confirmation or take-down.
- Full template fields, edge cases, and withdrawal rules below.

## Before shoot / interview / publish

Before you press record or publish, clarify **what you want**, **who is affected**, and **what they agreed to**.

### Concrete

- What **media** (photo, video, audio, quote, name) do you need?
- Which **channels** (print, web, social, third-party press)?
- **Duration** (one campaign, three years, archive)?
- **Identifiability**: full face, back-of-head, silhouette, voice-only, anonymised?
- **Language and literacy** of the person signing?

### Meta

- The power imbalance (journalist/org vs. subject) is real. Err on the side of **less** disclosure and **more** control for the person.
- A signed form does not override dignity. If the person later regrets it and the piece is not legally essential, take it down.

---

## Purpose

Make image and quote releases a **craft, not a form**: real consent, proper records, and a clear path for someone to withdraw.

---

## 1. What the release must cover

- **Who**: full name and contact of the person (or guardian).
- **What**: photos, video, audio, quotes, combined with name yes/no, organisation yes/no.
- **Where**: channels (org website, social, print leaflet, annual report, press hand-out, third-party press).
- **How long**: one-off, three years, indefinite, "until project end".
- **Languages / translations**: German only, German + English, translation into other languages allowed?
- **Modifications**: crop, recolour, edit—allowed or not?
- **Withdrawal**: how, where, within what timeline.
- **Signature**, date, witness where relevant.

## 2. The "specific" bar

Vague releases ("for our work") fail. Good phrasing names:

- the **project** or **campaign**
- the **type of media** ("photo portrait and a quote of up to three sentences")
- the **channels** with examples ("our website ngo.example, our Instagram, and a printed annual report 2026")
- the **timeframe** ("until the end of 2028, then deleted")

## 3. Informed means readable

- Use **plain language** (see [`easy-read-english.md`](../language-&-communication/easy-read-english.md)).
- Offer the release in the person's **first language** where feasible.
- Explain what "online" means practically—screenshots, re-sharing, search results.
- Mention that even after deletion from your channels, copies may exist elsewhere.

## 4. Minors

- Always get the **guardian's** written consent.
- Get the **child's assent** in age-appropriate language, and respect a no.
- Avoid full faces and full names for children in sensitive contexts (health, family, legal); use a first name or initial.
- School/care-setting consent is **not transferable** to your comms—get your own.

## 5. Vulnerable adults

- Includes people in acute crisis, medical settings, shelters, detention, addiction care, or with capacity issues.
- Consent must be **at the right moment** (not in acute distress), with time to change their mind.
- A support worker or independent advocate should be present where appropriate.
- If in doubt, **anonymise**.

## 6. What "identifiable" really means

- A back-of-head shot plus first name plus the neighbourhood is identifiable.
- A voice-only clip plus role ("the priest in X") is identifiable.
- A story with three unique details (age, condition, location) is identifiable.
- Anonymisation is **combinatorial**—remove enough details, not just the face.

## 7. Workflow before publishing

1. **Explain** the project and what consent means.
2. **Show** examples of similar previous work.
3. **Sign** the release (digital or paper).
4. **Offer** the person a copy of the final piece before publication where practical.
5. **File** the release with the asset—a photo without a matching release is not a photo you own rights to.

## 8. Withdrawal and take-down

- Provide **one clear contact** for withdrawal.
- Act **quickly**—days, not months—for online channels.
- For print already distributed, document the decision; avoid reprinting.
- Withdrawals are not negotiations. Do not push back on a person changing their mind.

## 9. Third-party press and re-use

- Releases for your org do **not** automatically extend to news outlets republishing your material. Say so in the release, or negotiate explicitly.
- Re-use for a **different campaign** requires a fresh release if the context has changed meaningfully.

## 10. Red flags

- "We can sort the paperwork later, just film now."
- Guardians signing without the child present or without explanation.
- Pressure from donors/partners to name or show someone the person is not comfortable with.
- Emotionally charged moments (funerals, hospital rooms, court hallways) used for consent.

---

## Core idea

A release is **a promise you can keep**, not a lawyer's shield. If you would not be comfortable being shown and quoted like this, neither are they—rewrite the release, or don't publish.
