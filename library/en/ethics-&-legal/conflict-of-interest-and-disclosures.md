# Conflict of interest and disclosures

**Scope:** Applies to **identifying and disclosing conflicts** in charity, research, journalism, and product contexts—gifts, paid work, family, shares, advocacy, AI/vendor relationships. Not anti-corruption law in full, not regulatory bribery frameworks. Pair with [`ai-disclosure-and-policy.md`](ai-disclosure-and-policy.md), [`respectful-language.md`](../language-&-communication/respectful-language.md), [`status-updates-and-reporting.md`](../project-&-operations/status-updates-and-reporting.md), and [`image-and-quote-releases.md`](image-and-quote-releases.md).

## Excerpt
- **Disclose early, disclose in writing, disclose specifically.**
- **A conflict is not wrongdoing**; unmanaged conflict is.
- **Register** conflicts annually and at the point of a decision—both.
- **Recuse** from the decision, not just the final vote.
- **Appearance** matters—"could a reasonable outsider see this as conflicted?" is a real test.
- Sample language and patterns below.

## Before a decision

### Concrete

- Do you or close family have a **financial** interest?
- Do you have **past work** with a bidder/partner/person being hired?
- Do you hold a **board seat**, **advisory role**, or **friendship** that could influence judgement?
- Have you received **gifts, hospitality, or payments** that relate to the decision?
- Are you using **AI tools or vendors** where you stand to benefit from their selection?

### Meta

- If you have to think twice, **disclose**. It is usually cheap; silence is expensive.
- Conflicts evolve—what was fine last year may not be now. Re-declare.

---

## Purpose

Keep decisions **trustworthy, defensible, and fair**—and protect people from awkward accusations that would not have existed with a simple disclosure.

---

## 1. What counts as a conflict

- **Financial** — shares, ownership, consulting fees, board fees, royalties.
- **Personal** — family, partners, close friendships, caring relationships.
- **Professional** — past employment, joint projects, ongoing collaborations.
- **Positional** — other board seats, volunteer roles, political affiliations.
- **Competing duties** — two clients with opposing interests.
- **Gifts and hospitality** — meals, trips, tickets, speaking fees.
- **Intellectual** — strong prior public position on the very question being decided.

All can be legitimate; they must be **visible**.

## 2. Register early, register specifically

Annual register + decision-time declaration.

### Bad: register early, register specifically

```
"I have some business with them, but it's fine."
```

### Good: register early, register specifically

```
Declared interest (2025 register):
- My partner is Head of Marketing at VendorX.
- I hold 50 shares in VendorY (<£500).
- I am a trustee of CharityZ (unpaid).
- I spoke at a conference sponsored by VendorX (travel paid, no fee).
```

The register should have dates, roles, amounts where meaningful, and a review schedule.

## 3. At the decision point

Even if you declared in the register, **restate** at the meeting or in the document:

```
At the start of today's procurement decision:
Alex discloses that their partner works at one of the bidders (VendorX).
Alex will leave the room for the discussion and the vote.
This disclosure is recorded in the minutes.
```

Rules:

- **Before** the discussion, not after.
- **Written** in minutes or in the relevant document.
- **Action**: recuse, advise-only, or proceed with mitigations.

## 4. Recusal done properly

- Leave the **room / thread / document**.
- Do not **read** the materials after the fact.
- Do not **lobby** colleagues informally.
- Have a **clear record** of who made the decision.

Half-recusal ("I'll stay but not vote") rarely works—influence is not just votes.

## 5. Mitigations when recusal isn't possible

Sometimes the conflicted person is the only one with expertise.

- Hire an **independent reviewer** to check the decision.
- Document **why** the person was involved and what was done to balance it.
- Split the decision into **criteria** (set by uninvolved people) and **evaluation** (involved person explains facts, uninvolved people score).

## 6. Gifts and hospitality

- **Low-value** items can often be accepted; keep a register.
- **Higher-value** items (conference travel, tickets, speaker fees) need approval.
- **Never** accept anything linked to an active procurement or decision you influence.
- **Return** gifts that arrive unexpectedly and could look like influence.

Thresholds differ by country, sector, and org—write yours down.

## 7. Journalism, research, and advocacy

- **Journalists** disclose relationships that could affect coverage ("the author's partner works for X").
- **Researchers** disclose funders, sponsors, in-kind support, and advisory roles.
- **Advocates** disclose advocacy positions that precede a "neutral" analysis.
- Pair with [`source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md`](../research-&-analysis/source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md) and [`op-eds-and-bylined-articles.md`](../language-&-communication/op-eds-and-bylined-articles.md).

## 8. AI-specific disclosures

- **Using** a tool you own shares in (recommend it cautiously, disclose).
- **Procuring** an AI vendor where a staff member has prior paid work with them (declare).
- **Publishing** content materially generated by AI (disclose per [`ai-disclosure-and-policy.md`](ai-disclosure-and-policy.md)).
- **Training data** from interested parties (mention in research or product docs).

AI amplifies speed; conflicts amplify reputational risk. Disclose both.

## 9. Handling a missed or late disclosure

- **Acknowledge** promptly: "I should have declared X in last week's decision."
- **Assess** the impact—was the decision materially different?
- **Revisit** if the decision is still reversible.
- **Learn**: update the register process so the same gap does not happen again.

Quiet later-disclosures are how small issues become big ones.

## 10. What not to do

- Rely on **oral** declarations that nobody writes down.
- Declare "general awareness" without **specifics**.
- Stay in the meeting "because I recused myself from voting".
- Use **NDAs** to avoid declaring to your own board.
- Treat disclosures as a **box-tick**; read the register before big decisions.

---

## Core idea

Conflicts are **normal**; concealed conflicts corrode trust. Write them down, declare them early, recuse fully, and revisit regularly. The goal is not purity—it is **decisions a third party would find defensible**.
