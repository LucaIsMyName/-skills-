# AI disclosure and policy

**Scope:** Applies to **using AI in communications work**—drafting, translating, editing, generating images, summarising, transcribing. Not a replacement for an org-wide AI policy or legal review. Pair with [`data-protection-for-comms.md`](data-protection-for-comms.md), [`copyright-and-usage-rights.md`](copyright-and-usage-rights.md), and [`respectful-language.md`](../language-&-communication/respectful-language.md).

## Excerpt 

- **AI is a tool**, not an author. The human publishing the piece stays responsible for accuracy, consent, and tone.
- **Disclose meaningful AI use** to the reader. "We used spell-check" is not material; "this image was fully AI-generated" is.
- **Never paste** personal data, safeguarding cases, donor records, or client quotes into AI tools you have not cleared.
- **Label synthetic media** (images, voices, videos) clearly—especially when they might be mistaken for a real person.
- Full disclosure rules, prompt hygiene, and red flags below.

## Before using AI on comms

Before using an AI tool in a piece of work, clarify **why you are using it, what you give it, and what you owe the reader**.

### Concrete

- Which **tool** and **model** (name, version, vendor)?
- What **data** will go in (personal data, confidential, public)?
- How much of the **final output** is AI-shaped (outline only, heavy edit, near-verbatim)?
- Does the output need **attribution** to sources the model may have paraphrased?

### Meta

- If disclosing would embarrass you, the problem is **use**, not disclosure.
- "Faster" is not a reason to skip safeguards.

## Purpose

Set a clear, calm policy for AI in communications that protects the people in the work, informs the reader, and keeps the org honest.

---

## 1. Principles

- **Human responsibility** for every published piece.
- **Honest disclosure** proportional to AI involvement.
- **No personal or sensitive data** into unvetted tools.
- **No synthetic people** passed off as real.
- **Source-aware**: if the model paraphrased someone, check and credit.

## 2. When to disclose

Disclose when AI **shaped the content meaningfully**, e.g.:

- **Image / illustration / voice / video** generated or substantially altered by AI.
- **Text** where more than small edits came from AI (translated long passages, ghost-drafted).
- **Summaries** of AI-analysed data that feed into conclusions.

You do **not** need to disclose:

- Spell-check, grammar-check, style suggestions in the author's voice.
- Caption auto-suggestions you reviewed and edited.
- Search queries.

## 3. How to disclose

- **Plain sentence near the asset**: "This image was generated with [tool]." or "Translated with AI support and edited by the team."
- **On images**: visible caption or in the credits; do not hide in a tooltip.
- **On audio/video**: mention in show notes or at the end.
- **On long-form pieces with mixed authorship**: a clear note in the byline area.

## 4. What never goes into a third-party AI

- **Personal data** (names, emails, addresses, IDs, photos of identifiable people) unless the tool is covered by a DPA and the use is cleared.
- **Safeguarding / case material** (see [`safeguarding-in-comms.md`](safeguarding-in-comms.md)).
- **Confidential** strategy, donor lists, HR matters.
- **Quotes under embargo** or releases limited to your channels.
- **Credentials** (API keys, passwords).

Assume free tools can use your input for training unless the terms say otherwise.

## 5. Prompt hygiene

- Strip personal data before pasting; work with roles and anonymised scenarios.
- Prefer **self-hosted** or **enterprise** tiers for anything sensitive.
- Keep prompts and outputs with the asset for traceability—comms rights logs (see [`copyright-and-usage-rights.md`](copyright-and-usage-rights.md)) include the AI fields.

## 6. Synthetic people and voices

- **Do not** generate an image that could be mistaken for a real identifiable person without explicit, informed consent.
- **Do not** clone a real voice without a signed release for that specific use.
- Mark **composite** or **illustrative** photos as such; never caption them as if they were documentary.
- For **survivors' stories** told via avatars/voices, treat synthetic stand-ins as a safeguarding decision, not a creative one.

## 7. Accuracy and hallucinations

- AI-generated text can invent facts, quotes, laws, and citations. **Verify every factual claim** against a trusted source before publication.
- For press and legal copy, assume AI drafts are **starting points**, not final text.
- Keep a **two-human rule** on sensitive pieces: one to draft, one to check.

## 8. Accessibility and plain language

- AI can help draft plain-language versions; still run them through a human check against [`easy-read-english.md`](../language-&-communication/easy-read-english.md).
- Auto-captions and auto-transcripts are **drafts**, not final captions. Edit them.

## 9. Editorial independence

- AI output should not dictate framing for affected communities—see [`respectful-language.md`](../language-&-communication/respectful-language.md).
- If the tool consistently biases against a group (terms, imagery), stop using it for that work.

## 10. Red flags

- "Make it sound like [real person] wrote it."
- "Generate a victim-like image we can use as a case study."
- Disclosure only in a footer nobody reads.
- Pasting a real interview transcript into a free AI tool.
- Publishing AI translations in a language nobody on the team speaks.

---

## Core idea

The question is never "did you use AI". It is "**were you honest about it, and did the people in the work come to harm**". Disclose, protect, verify—in that order.
