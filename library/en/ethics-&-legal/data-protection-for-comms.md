# Data protection for comms

**Scope:** Applies to **GDPR-style data protection** as it affects communications work (photos, quotes, newsletters, forms, analytics). Not a legal opinion—when in doubt, escalate to a DPO or lawyer. Pair with [`image-and-quote-releases.md`](image-and-quote-releases.md), [`safeguarding-in-comms.md`](safeguarding-in-comms.md), and [`newsletter-and-email.md`](../language-&-communication/newsletter-and-email.md).

## Excerpt 

- **Personal data** is anything that can identify a person directly or in combination (name, photo, email, voice, IP).
- **Lawful basis first**, then process: usually **consent**, **contract**, or **legitimate interest** for comms; **special categories** (health, religion, sexuality) need a higher bar.
- **Minimise**: collect the least, keep it shortest, share it narrowest.
- **Document** consent, retention, and sharing—"we told them verbally" is not a record.
- Full rules, examples, and the comms-side checklist are below.

## AI / prompt: before publishing

Before publishing anything involving a real person, clarify **lawful basis, scope, and retention**. Do not assume old consent covers new uses.

### Concrete

- **Who** is identifiable and **how** (name, face, quote, location, org, role)?
- **Lawful basis** for this use (consent, legitimate interest, contract, legal obligation)?
- **Scope** of consent: channels, duration, languages, third parties?
- **Retention**: when is this deleted, and by whom?

### Meta

- Dignity and law point the same way more often than not—when they diverge, dignity wins editorially, law wins on publication.
- When uncertain, **ask**. Comms is not the place to improvise data protection.

## Purpose

Give comms teams a **practical** data-protection floor: what counts as personal data, how consent works, what to document, and what never to publish without checking.

---

## 1. What counts as personal data

- **Direct**: name, email, phone, postal address, ID number, face on a photo, recognisable voice.
- **Indirect/combined**: role + small-town org ("the social worker in Kleinheim"), birthday + street, rare diagnosis + first name.
- **Special categories** (extra protection): health, religion, political views, union membership, ethnic origin, sex life, biometric/genetic data.
- **Children's data** is always sensitive; consent thresholds vary by country (often 16 in the EU, 13 in the US).

## 2. Lawful basis in practice

- **Consent**: specific, informed, freely given, revocable. Written, with what was said and when.
- **Contract**: to deliver a service the person has requested (e.g. welcome email for a course they signed up to).
- **Legitimate interest**: internal analytics, fraud prevention; requires a balancing test and an opt-out.
- **Legal obligation**: tax, reporting, safeguarding reports.

Newsletters in the EU generally need **opt-in consent**, not a pre-ticked box and not "because they gave us a business card".

## 3. Minimisation

- Collect **only what you need** for the stated purpose.
- If you do not know why you need a field, **remove it** from the form.
- Shorten retention: newsletter bounces older than X months, event lists after the event + reporting window, photos after project end unless there is archive justification.

## 4. Consent for comms

- **Specific**: what will we do with the photo/quote/data?
- **Informed**: in plain language (pair with [`easy-read-english.md`](../language-&-communication/easy-read-english.md)).
- **Freely given**: no "consent or you cannot attend".
- **Revocable**: easy, documented, in the same channel or better.
- **Recorded**: date, wording, signatory or tick-box log.

See [`image-and-quote-releases.md`](image-and-quote-releases.md) for the release-form specifics.

## 5. Newsletters and transactional email

- **Double opt-in** is the safe default in the EU.
- **Unsubscribe** in every mail; one click, no login required.
- **Imprint / legal line**: provider, address, privacy link.
- **No pre-ticked boxes**, no bundled consent ("I agree to terms and marketing").

## 6. Analytics, pixels, cookies

- Track only with a **lawful basis**; in the EU that usually means consent via a real cookie banner (not a dark pattern).
- **Cookie banner** has "Reject" as visible and easy as "Accept".
- Server-side analytics, anonymised logs, and privacy-respecting tools (Plausible, Fathom) reduce risk.

## 7. Sharing with third parties

- Hosting, email, CRM, video, transcription, AI services—all are **data processors** and need a **data processing agreement (DPA)**.
- Transfers **outside the EU/EEA** need a valid mechanism (adequacy decision, SCCs).
- Do not paste personal data into free AI tools unless you know what happens to it.

## 8. Breach response

- Any **unauthorised access, loss, or disclosure** is a breach—lost laptop, wrong email list, misconfigured bucket, phishing hit.
- Inform the DPO/lead **within hours**. In the EU, the authority usually needs notice **within 72 hours**.
- Document: what happened, who is affected, what data, what mitigation.

## 9. What never goes out without checking

- Photos of children, hospital patients, residents, clients.
- Quotes from named individuals in sensitive contexts (addiction, migration status, abuse, mental health).
- Employee information beyond name + role.
- Case studies with enough detail to re-identify someone small-town.

## 10. Comms checklist (print this)

- [ ] Is there a **lawful basis** for every person named or shown?
- [ ] Is **consent** documented, specific, and current?
- [ ] Is this the **minimum data** needed?
- [ ] Is **retention** set and enforceable?
- [ ] Are **third parties** covered by a DPA?
- [ ] Is the **unsubscribe / withdrawal** path obvious?
- [ ] If something goes wrong, **who** gets the call and **how fast**?

## Core idea

Data protection in comms is not a paperwork ritual—it is how you treat the people in your stories. Minimise, document, and when in doubt **ask before publishing**.
