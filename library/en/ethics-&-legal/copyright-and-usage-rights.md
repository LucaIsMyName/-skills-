# Copyright and usage rights

**Scope:** Applies to **what you may use, adapt, and publish**—photos, illustrations, music, fonts, code snippets, text quotes, and AI-generated material. Not a substitute for legal advice on complex licensing disputes. Pair with [`image-and-quote-releases.md`](image-and-quote-releases.md) and [`ai-disclosure-and-policy.md`](ai-disclosure-and-policy.md).

## Excerpt 

- **Default: everything is copyrighted** to whoever made it, from the moment it is created.
- **Licence first, use second.** Check licence terms (scope, attribution, modifications, commercial use) before downloading or pasting.
- **Attribution is not a licence**—crediting someone does not make unlicensed use legal.
- **AI-generated material** has unsettled legal status; treat outputs as uncertain and keep prompts/sources documented.
- Full rules, licence types, and nonprofit-specific notes below.

## Before using someone else's work

Before reusing any asset, clarify **who owns it, what the licence says, and what you actually plan to do**.

### Concrete

- **Source**: who made it, where is the licence text?
- **Licence type**: all rights reserved, CC-BY, CC-BY-SA, CC0, public domain, commercial stock, custom?
- **Planned use**: channels, modifications, derivative works, commercial vs. non-commercial?
- **Attribution** required format and placement?

### Meta

- "I found it on Google Images" is not a licence.
- When in doubt, **ask the creator** or pick a clearly licensed alternative.

## Purpose

Keep the comms pipeline **clean of rights problems**: know what you are using, keep evidence, and give credit where the licence requires it.

## 1. Default rules

- Original creative work (photo, text, illustration, video, music, code) is **automatically copyrighted** on creation—no registration needed.
- Copyright typically lasts **decades beyond the creator's lifetime** (length varies by country).
- **Facts and ideas** are not copyrightable; their **expression** is.

## 2. Common licences at a glance

- **All rights reserved**: nothing allowed without explicit permission.
- **CC0 / Public Domain**: use freely, no attribution needed (but good manners to credit).
- **CC-BY**: use freely, **credit the author** and link the licence.
- **CC-BY-SA**: CC-BY plus **share-alike**—derivatives must carry the same licence.
- **CC-BY-NC**: non-commercial only. Most nonprofit campaigns with donor/funder money count as commercial in practice—check.
- **CC-BY-ND**: no derivatives. You may not crop or recolour meaningfully.
- **Commercial stock** (Getty, Adobe, etc.): strict licence terms, often per-seat and per-channel; read the agreement.
- **Open fonts** (SIL OFL): use in documents and apps; re-selling or rebranding the font itself is usually forbidden.
- **Code licences** (MIT, Apache-2.0, GPL, AGPL): scope your use—GPL/AGPL have strong copyleft requirements.

## 3. Attribution done right

For CC-BY the usual TASL pattern: **T**itle, **A**uthor, **S**ource, **L**icence (linked).

Example:

```
Photo: "Market day" by J. Example, via Unsplash-like source, CC-BY-4.0 (with link).
```

Place attribution **near the asset** or in a clearly linked credits section.

## 4. Modifications and derivatives

- ND licences forbid meaningful edits; a crop for format is usually fine, a recolour plus composite probably is not.
- SA licences require the **derivative** to carry the same licence and the same share-alike clause—be sure your donor/partner contract allows that.
- Mark derivatives clearly: "adapted from …, licensed under …".

## 5. Text quotes and press clippings

- Short quotations for commentary, criticism, or reporting are generally allowed under **quotation rights / fair dealing / fair use**—rules differ by country.
- Full-text reproduction of someone else's article is **not** allowed without a licence or press agreement.
- Always credit the source and link where possible.

## 6. Photos of public people, events, and places

- Public figures doing public acts: usually fine to publish; check local rules (esp. Germany's "Kunsturhebergesetz" §22/§23).
- Buildings from public space: usually fine (panorama freedom); interiors often not.
- **Children, patients, clients**: consent-based, not location-based.

## 7. Fonts, icons, sounds

- Verify the licence covers your **use case** (web, print, app, video embed).
- Many "free for personal use" fonts are **not free for org use**.
- Icon sets: check attribution requirements and whether you can include them in a product you ship.

## 8. AI-generated content

- Legal status is unsettled in most jurisdictions; outputs may not be protectable, and in some jurisdictions may be restricted from use.
- **Prompt inputs matter**: do not paste confidential or personal data into tools you have not cleared.
- Keep a record of **model, prompt, date, and the final asset**.
- Disclose AI use per [`ai-disclosure-and-policy.md`](ai-disclosure-and-policy.md).

## 9. Nonprofit-specific traps

- "Non-commercial" is narrower than it sounds; fundraising often disqualifies you.
- Donor logos and partner marks are trademark-protected; use them only under the partner's style guide.
- Employee-generated content (photos, designs) often belongs to the **org**, but external volunteers need a separate agreement.

## 10. Documentation

- Keep a **rights log**: asset, source, licence, date, who cleared it, where it is used.
- Store the **licence text** or a link alongside the asset, not just in someone's head.
- Review when a campaign ends—retire assets whose licences have lapsed.

---

## Core idea

Clean copyright practice is **boring on purpose**: read the licence, credit the creator, write it down. Exciting copyright stories happen to people who did none of that.
