# Notes and synthesis

**Scope:** Applies to **taking, organising, and turning research notes into drafts**—for articles, briefings, reports, and stories. Pair with [`desk-research.md`](desk-research.md), [`interviewing-people.md`](interviewing-people.md), [`literature-summarising.md`](literature-summarising.md), [`source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md`](source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md).

## Excerpt 

- **Notes are for thinking**, not for memory alone. Capture ideas, not just quotes.
- **One fact, one note**—atomic notes beat long transcripts when it is time to draft.
- **Provenance always**: every note links back to its source and page/timestamp.
- **Synthesis is a separate step**: you rearrange, combine, and draw conclusions from atomic notes, not from raw material.
- **Draft from the synthesis**, not from the notes file—do not paste-and-polish.
- Full system and workflow below.

## Before taking notes

Before opening the notebook, clarify **why you are noting and what you will do with the notes**.

### Concrete

- **Purpose**: brief, article, report, story, talk?
- **Deliverable length** and audience?
- **What you already believe** going in (your priors)?
- **What would change your mind**?

### Meta

- Notes are **not** a transcript. Summarise and capture intent.
- Your future self is the audience. Write so they can rebuild the context.

## Purpose

Turn a pile of inputs—interviews, studies, reports, web pages—into a **confident, well-sourced draft** without losing traceability.

---

## 1. A simple note system

- **Source note**: metadata for each source (see [`desk-research.md`](desk-research.md) log).
- **Literature note**: a short, original-language paraphrase of what the source says, per idea.
- **Atomic note**: one idea, one file or card—in your own words, linked back to the source.
- **Map / outline note**: arranges atomic notes into a structure for a deliverable.

(Names differ: Zettelkasten, PARA, BASB. Pick one, then stop shopping for systems.)

## 2. Taking notes during reading

- Read **once** for shape (skim), **again** for substance (note).
- Capture: **claim**, **evidence**, **source page/time**, **your reaction**.
- Distinguish **quote** (`"…"`) from **paraphrase** (your words) from **comment** (your thinking).
- **Color- or symbol-code** for speed: `?` unclear, `!` important, `→` follow-up, `//` my comment.

## 3. Taking notes during interviews

- Record; take **sparse** notes live (see [`interviewing-people.md`](interviewing-people.md)).
- **Timestamp** notable quotes; jot spelling of names and numbers.
- Note **non-verbal**: pauses, laughter, mood shift.
- Immediately after: write a **2-minute debrief** while it's fresh—what surprised you, what you still need.

## 4. Atomic notes

- One note = one idea, in **your own words**, 1–5 sentences.
- **Title** the note as a claim: "Waiting lists in Saxony lengthened 40% since 2021", not "Saxony data".
- End with a **link to the source** and the exact locator (page, section, timestamp).
- Tag sparingly; two or three tags max.

## 5. Turning notes into a structure

- **Cluster**: lay atomic notes on a table/board; group by theme.
- **Rank**: mark must-include vs. nice-to-have vs. drop.
- **Sequence**: arrange into a spine—what does the reader need first, next, last?
- **Find gaps**: weak evidence, missing counter-argument, unverified claim.
- **Log** the gaps as explicit to-dos before drafting.

## 6. Synthesis, not stitching

- Write a **first pass summary** in your own words, with notes hidden.
- Drop in **evidence** from atomic notes, not long quotes.
- If a section has only one source, flag it and find a second before continuing.
- Add **counter-evidence** explicitly—do not skip it because it complicates the story.

## 7. Drafting from synthesis

- Start from the **outline**, not the notes file.
- Aim for **one claim per paragraph**, with evidence and source.
- Keep a **claims log** alongside the draft: which sentences assert facts, where they are sourced.
- When you cannot remember where a fact came from, **stop and find it**—do not fudge with "research shows".

## 8. Quotes: use sparingly

- Use a quote when the **voice** matters (person's own words, striking phrasing, authoritative framing).
- Otherwise paraphrase and attribute.
- Long quotes = the source is doing the thinking. You should be.

## 9. Version control for notes

- Keep originals **raw**; work on copies when you synthesise.
- Mark up drafts with change notes: **added claim**, **removed unsourced line**, **merged notes 12+17**.
- For collaborative work, agree on a convention (comments vs. suggestions vs. direct edits).

## 10. AI assistants

- AI can **cluster**, **summarise draft sections**, **find gaps**—treat all output as suggestions.
- **Never** trust AI quotes, page numbers, or citations without verifying against the source.
- Keep the **prompts and outputs** with the project files (see [`ai-disclosure-and-policy.md`](../ethics-safeguarding-legal/ai-disclosure-and-policy.md)).
- The synthesis is **yours**; the AI is scaffolding.

## 11. Red flags

- A notes file that is really a giant transcript.
- Draft paragraphs where you cannot say where each claim came from.
- "Interesting!" highlights with no summary—future-you won't remember why.
- Merged files with lost source attributions.
- Tags that multiply into a second system to manage.

---

## Core idea

Good research becomes good writing through **atomic notes and honest synthesis**. Capture in your own words, link every idea to a source, rearrange before you write—and draft from the map, not from the pile.
