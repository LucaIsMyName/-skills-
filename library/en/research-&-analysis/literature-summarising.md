# Literature summarising

**Scope:** Applies to **condensing studies, reports, policy papers, and long articles** into honest, attributed summaries for briefings, stories, and internal notes. Pair with [`source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md`](source-evaluation-and-fact-checking.md), [`notes-and-synthesis.md`](notes-and-synthesis.md), [`reading-statistics-plainly.md`](reading-statistics-plainly.md).

## Excerpt 

- **Faithfulness first**: a summary preserves the source's claim, scope, and uncertainty—then trims.
- **Keep the hedges.** "May", "in some cases", "limited to a small sample"—these are the source's truth, not filler to delete.
- **Separate the finding from your take.** Summary and commentary go in different paragraphs.
- **Quote sparingly and exactly.** Paraphrase most things; use quotes only where wording matters.
- **Name the source up front**: who, when, what type, where published. Then summarise.
- Full workflow and traps below.

## Before summarising

Before condensing any source, clarify **why, for whom, and how much**.

### Concrete

- **Reader**: lay, expert, funder, internal team?
- **Length** target (100, 300, 1,000 words)?
- **What the reader needs**: headline finding, methods, caveats, implications?
- **What the source actually is**: peer-reviewed, policy paper, advocacy brief, journalism?

### Meta

- Summaries lose fidelity—pick what to lose consciously.
- If you cannot say what the source does **not** claim, you have not read closely enough.

## Purpose

Produce summaries that a reader can **trust**: true to the source's meaning, transparent about limitations, and clearly separated from your interpretation.

---

## 1. Before you summarise

- Read the **abstract, introduction, and conclusion** first for shape.
- Scan **methods and limitations**—the story often lives there.
- Note the **author(s), year, type, funder**.
- Check the **date**—is this still current policy/evidence?
- Decide the **angle**: the reader's question, not the source's own agenda.

## 2. Structure of a short summary

A reliable 150–300 word template:

1. **Source line**: "A 2025 OECD report on X, by A and B, finds that…"
2. **Headline finding** in plain language (1–2 sentences).
3. **Scope and method**: what the source did, sample, geography, period (1–2 sentences).
4. **Key caveats**: sample limits, contested definitions, regional differences.
5. **Implication** (if appropriate to the deliverable), framed clearly as inference, not finding.

## 3. Faithfulness checks

- **Hedging words** preserved (may, some, often).
- **Numbers** carry their unit and basis.
- **Scope** is clear (population, geography, period).
- **Limitations** the source itself lists are mentioned.
- **Dissenting view** inside the paper is included if it was central.

## 4. Separating finding from commentary

- Summary paragraph: what the source says.
- Commentary paragraph: what you think about it, what is missing, what it means for your audience.
- Mark the shift visibly ("Commentary:", "Our take:").

## 5. Quoting and paraphrasing

- **Paraphrase** is the default. Use your own words; cite the source.
- **Quote** when the phrasing itself is the point (precise definition, charged wording, memorable image).
- Never insert quote marks around a paraphrase.
- Check every quote against the source, word-for-word.

## 6. Numbers

- Cite numbers with **definition + unit + year + source**.
- Prefer **absolute + relative** together for risk ("2 extra cases per 10,000, a 30% increase").
- See [`reading-statistics-plainly.md`](reading-statistics-plainly.md) for common traps.

## 7. Tone and register

- Match the reader, not the author. A peer-reviewed paper written for scientists still needs plain-language summaries for a general audience—see [`easy-read-english.md`](../language-&-communication/easy-read-english.md).
- **Do not sharpen claims** to make them more quotable. Softening ("according to the authors") is often closer to the truth than a bare assertion.
- **Do not soften** uncomfortable findings either. Faithfulness cuts both ways.

## 8. Writing under tight word counts

- **Cut adjectives and throat-clearing** first.
- **Collapse examples**: pick the one clearest example; drop the rest.
- **Combine sentences** that share a subject.
- Keep the **hedges and units**—they cost few words and carry a lot.

## 9. Multiple sources in one summary

- **Group** by agreement: "Two reports (A, B) find… A third (C) disagrees because…"
- **Note** the type of source for each (peer-reviewed vs. advocacy vs. government).
- **Weigh**: a single well-done study does not outweigh a systematic review—say so.
- Summaries that hide disagreement are misleading; name it.

## 10. AI-assisted summarising

- AI can **draft** a summary; you are responsible for the final version.
- Verify **every claim, number, quote, and citation** against the source yourself.
- Watch for **fabricated** page numbers and authors—frequent in AI output.
- Keep **prompt and output** with project notes; see [`ai-disclosure-and-policy.md`](../ethics-&-legal/ai-disclosure-and-policy.md).

## 11. Red flags

- A summary that is more confident than the paper.
- Numbers with no year or unit.
- Missing funding/conflict-of-interest disclosure for advocacy material.
- "Studies show…" with no specific study.
- Sharpened phrasing that turns "may correlate" into "causes".

---

## Core idea

A good summary is **shorter, not bolder**. Preserve what the source actually claims, keep the hedges, attribute clearly, separate your commentary—and the reader can trust both the summary and the source behind it.
