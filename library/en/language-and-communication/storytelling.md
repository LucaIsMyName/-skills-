# Storytelling

**Scope:** Effective storytelling (structure, emotion, clarity) for copy, campaigns, and speeches; **not** a substitute for **approved reporting on real people**—use [`writing-a-story.md`](writing-a-story.md), [`respectful-language.md`](respectful-language.md), and optionally [`heroes-journey.md`](heroes-journey.md).

## Excerpt (read this first)

- **Five elements:** protagonist, place, plot, symbolic object, facts & background; the **narrator** conveys facts, guides attention, draws connections—use care with interpretation and speculation.
- Plus the **ladder of narration**, the **core narrative sentence**, and dramaturgy (three-act structure, short form of the hero’s journey).
- People follow **feelings, not bare facts**—a story shows **change** and **connections**.
- For real-life stories, **ask** about facts, consent, and the organisation’s role as **enabler**, not “hero.”
- All rules, Bad/Good examples, and the core idea are **fully** in the rest of this file.

## AI / prompt: completeness before drafting

Before developing stories, clarify **concrete and meta** inputs. If something essential is missing, **ask**—do not invent scenes, quotes, or biographical details.

### Concrete

- **Audience** and **channel** (article, social, speech, video concept)
- For **real people:** facts, **approvals**, quotes, anonymisation rules
- **Organisation** (NGO/social): only as **supporter/mentor** in the enabler sense—no invented impact claims

### Meta

- Desired **emotional** depth (authentic, not manipulative)
- Apply **Marie Lampert** structure deliberately: core sentence and “ladder”

## Purpose

This document describes how **effective storytelling after Marie Lampert** works—with a focus on structure, emotion, and clarity.

The goal is to tell stories that:

- **reach people emotionally**
- make **connections** understandable
- make **change** tangible

👉 People follow **feelings, not facts**

---

### Good

- “The king dies—and shortly after, the queen dies of grief.”
  → connection + emotion

### Bad

- “The king dies. Then the queen dies.”
  → facts only, no story

---

## Core principle

### Stories show change

- A story is the **search for a lost balance**
- It shows:
  - cause → effect
  - development → change

---

### Good

- “After losing her job, she starts to find a new direction—and discovers another path.”
  → development + cause

### Bad

- “She loses her job and later works again.”
  → no connection, no depth

---

## The 5 core elements of a story

---

### 1. Protagonist

- Bridge between audience and topic
- needs:
  - desire / problem
  - personality
  - development
- may have **weaknesses** (makes them believable)

---

### Good

- “He wants to live independently but is afraid to decide alone.”
  → goal + weakness

### Bad

- “He is successful and masters every challenge effortlessly.”
  → no identification

---

### 2. Place

- creates:
  - atmosphere
  - context
  - a mental picture

---

### Good

- “The neon tube flickers; outside, the last tram goes by.”
  → atmosphere

### Bad

- “He is in a place.”
  → no effect

---

### 3. Plot

- core: **change**
- arises through:
  - conflicts
  - obstacles
- needs:
  - beginning, middle, end
  - causal links

---

### Good

- “He fails several times before he finds a solution.”
  → conflict + development

### Bad

- “He has challenges in life.”
  → no real plot

---

### 4. Object (symbolic detail)

- can:
  - open the story
  - be a red thread
  - carry meaning

---

### Good

- “The old key is still on her key ring.”
  → symbolic, concrete

### Bad

- no concrete detail
  → the story stays abstract

---

### 5. Facts & background

- support the story
- must **not dominate**

---

### Good

- Facts woven into the plot (“For three years he has been waiting for a place.”)

### Bad

- “Studies show 35% are affected.”
  → isolated, no link to the story

---

## The narrator

Besides protagonist and plot there is the **narrator** (the narrative voice)—not always the same as the author, but it shapes **how** the story is understood.

- **explains and conveys facts**—in the role of explainer, gives examples
- **stands in for readers** as a naive observer (asks the “naive” questions)
- **comments on and evaluates** what protagonists say
- **guides readers’ attention** and makes the story compelling
- **frames** what she witnesses and **names what stands out**
- **interprets** circumstances people describe
- **hypothesises** about why something is the way it is—keep clear what is **observation** and what is **conjecture** (no faux certainty)
- **draws connections** in what is narrated (e.g. **private citizen vs. district judge**—roles, rights, different angles on the same situation)

## From topic to story

### Mindset

- **collect ideas** first, then evaluate
- try different perspectives:
  - people
  - places
  - conflicts
  - details

---

### Good

- A mind map with several possible protagonists and situations

### Bad

- Locking onto one idea immediately with no exploration

---

## Writing vividly

### The “ladder of narration”

- top: abstract (“poverty”)
- bottom: concrete (“an empty fridge”)

👉 Aim: **tell as concretely as possible**

---

### Good

- “He opens the fridge—only half a loaf of bread is inside.”
  → concrete

### Bad

- “He lives in difficult circumstances.”
  → abstract

---

## The narrative sentence (core of the story)

Structure:

> My audience should experience how [protagonist] goes through [plot] and changes because of it

---

### Good

- “Readers should experience how Anna finds the courage to live alone.”
  → clear focus

### Bad

- “It’s about an important topic.”
  → no focus

---

## Dramaturgy

---

### 1. Three-act structure

- protagonist has a goal
- meets obstacles
- reaches (or changes) their goal

---

### Good

- “He wants to find work → gets rejections → finally finds a job.”
  → clear structure

### Bad

- “He looks for work and eventually it works out.”
  → no tension

---

### 2. Hero’s journey (short form)

- starting situation
- challenge
- transformation

---

### Good

- “She leaves her familiar world, struggles with uncertainty, and returns stronger.”
  → transformation

### Bad

- “She experiences something new.”
  → no development

---

### 3. Hero’s journey (typical mistakes)

---

### Bad

- no inner change
- no obstacles
- everything goes smoothly

---

## Role of organisations (enabler principle)

- The organisation is **not the hero**
- but:
  - supporter
  - mentor
  - enabler

---

### Good

- “The service helps him make his own decisions.”
  → support in the background

### Bad

- “The organisation solves every problem.”
  → organisation in the spotlight

---

## Language & effect

---

### 1. Emotional, but not manipulative

---

### Good

- “She hesitates before she answers.”
  → real emotion

### Bad

- “An unbelievably tragic and terrible situation.”
  → exaggerated

---

### 2. Concrete before abstract

---

### Good

- “Every morning he sits in the same seat on the bus.”
  → tangible

### Bad

- “His everyday life is shaped by routines.”
  → abstract

---

## Reduce complexity

---

### 1. Focus on few characters

---

### Good

- one clear main character

### Bad

- many people with no orientation

---

### 2. Clarity through selection

---

### Good

- only relevant details

### Bad

- telling everything → no structure

---

## Core idea

👉 A story arises from:

- **one character**
- **one problem**
- **one development**
- **one coherent thread**

---

### Good

- “We experience how a person changes through a challenge.”

### Bad

- “We list information about a topic.”
